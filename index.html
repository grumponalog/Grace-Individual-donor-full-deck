Section 02
Grace at
the center.

At the corner of Third Street and Bowman stands Grace Episcopal Church, rooted in one of the poorest, most devastated neighborhoods in Mansfield for over 150 years. Grace didn't stay by accident. The people who built Grace, and the people who inherited it, understood that a church isn't just a building for worship. Grace is a presence in a neighborhood.

For more than 25 years, that presence has taken the form of a food pantry. Residents of Richland County with incomes below 200% of the federal poverty line are welcome to receive support. No means test. No faith requirement. No barriers. Just food, offered with dignity and without condition.

Section 02 — Grace at the center.
More than food.

What happens at Grace extends beyond food. Partners from OhioHealth, the Mansfield-Richland County Public Library, and a network of social service organizations work alongside pantry staff, recognizing that individuals who come for groceries are often ready to access additional support. The pantry has built something rare: a place that meets people where they are, while connecting them to additional resources.

Mansfield's longest-running AA meeting has gathered in that same building for more than 80 years. Community forums, health screenings, and neighborhood organizing sessions have filled those rooms.

This is more than a food distribution program housed in a church. It's a community institution that has anchored its neighborhood for generations and will continue into the future.

Section 03
The mission is growing.
The building isn't.
  • No dedicated receiving dock. Trucks arrive with large-scale deliveries, but there is no proper receiving infrastructure.
  • Inability to accept all available food. Volunteers unload thousands of pounds of food by hand through spaces not designed for that volume.
  • Insufficient cold storage. Cold storage capacity is insufficient for the volume of fresh and perishable food available through partners.
  • Limited distribution space. The current space limits the ability to provide a dignified, choice-based experience for guests.

Section 04 · 01
Food insecurity in childhood
isn't something children
recover from.
The brain requires roughly 20% of the body's daily energy to develop normally. When that energy is consistently insufficient, the effects on attention, memory, and cognitive development are measurable and lasting. The families who come to Grace aren't just facing short-term need. Their children are in a window of development that doesn't reopen. Meeting that need today creates the stability required for long-term growth tomorrow.

Section 04 · 02
The food that could reach
families often doesn't,
not because it isn't there.
According to Ohio EPA's 2024 Compost Planning Report, nearly 930 tons of food scraps are captured and diverted to composting facilities in Richland County each year. That's roughly 1.86 million pounds, and it represents only what gets measured and diverted. The total food waste generated in the county is significantly higher. Even so, families across Mansfield go without. The problem isn't supply. It is infrastructure: the ability to receive, store, and distribute food safely, at scale, and with dignity determines whether that food reaches a family or is lost.

Section 04 · 03
Demand is growing at a pace
the current facility
can't match.
The pantry served nearly 50,000 individuals in 2025, a 25% increase over the prior year, and projects another 25% increase ahead. Without this investment, capacity will continue to fall behind rising demand. This is not a future problem. It is the current reality. For many households, access to consistent food is what creates the margin needed to pursue employment, education, and long-term self-sufficiency.

Planned Improvements

Dignified, choice-based distribution space

Redesigned space allows guests to move freely, select what they need, and be served in an environment built around dignity and choice.

Dedicated receiving dock with lift access

A covered dock with scissor lift enables safe, efficient unloading of full semi-truck deliveries.

Expanded cold storage capacity

Walk-in coolers and freezers increase capacity for fresh and perishable food, reducing loss and improving nutritional access.

Dedicated partner service areas

On-site space for health and social service partners enables coordinated support during a single visit.

Outdoor gathering and community space

Green space and a covered pavilion create opportunities for community connection and neighborhood engagement.

Commercial kitchen and improved dry storage

Expanded kitchen and optimized storage improve operational efficiency and support increased volume.

What this investment means for Mansfield
Four ways this
investment delivers.

Grace functions as essential community infrastructure. Other investments in education, workforce development, and health become more effective. A stable city requires a stable foundation. Grace helps provide it.

When food insecurity rises, the strain shows up elsewhere. In hospitals, social services, and emergency systems. When it is reduced, the strain decreases. A pantry operating at full capacity, alongside its partners, does more than serve individuals. It stabilizes the systems those individuals rely on.

This investment sends a clear signal. Where a community directs resources, especially toward overlooked neighborhoods, reflects what it values. Grace embodies that choice clearly: Mansfield is being rebuilt with its most vulnerable residents, not around them.

This investment secures the future. $1 million of the $2.5 million goal is designated to cover operations, staffing, and maintenance over time, not just construction. This reduces reliance on year-to-year fundraising and allows the pantry to serve future generations with consistency.

Section 09 — The Invitation
The invitation.

What is happening at Grace is not new. It has been built over time, through consistency, through presence, and through a daily commitment to meet people with dignity. This work was in motion long before this campaign, and will continue because it's rooted in something deeper than any single initiative.

The Grace Still Multiplies campaign does not begin new work. It is an invitation to strengthen and extend the work that is already being done, building the infrastructure needed to operate at full capacity and serve the next generation of Mansfield residents with consistency and care.

The impact of this investment may not be immediate or easily measured. Over time, it appears in children whose development stayed consistent, in families who found stability when they needed it most, and in neighborhoods strengthened by reliable support. These outcomes don't fit neatly into a report, but they shape a city over time.

The work is already moving. There is a place for you in it. We invite you to be part of what comes next.
